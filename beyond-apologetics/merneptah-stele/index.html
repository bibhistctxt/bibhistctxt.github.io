    The Merneptah Stele is well known in apologetic circles as the inscription that contains the oldest mention of ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù outside the Bible.     Discovery

    Historical context of the discovery

    Unlike the Tel Dan Stele which was discovered at what might have been the height of biblical minimalism, the Merneptah Stele was discovered at the beginning of the era of biblical archaeology. It was a time when polymaths and reverends, funded by wealthy sponsors, would set off, spade in hand, for the lands of the Ancient Near East with the aim of ‚Äúdigging up the Bible‚Äù.

    How it was discovered

    Mortuary Temple of Merneptah

    One of these guys was William Flinders Petrie, the Edwards Professor of Egyptian Archaeology and Philology at University College London.

    He‚Äôd made it his mission to excavate six temples on the west bank of the Nile at Thebes, opposite Luxor, on the other side of the hill from the famous Valley of the Kings.

    Anyway, on December 16th, he got digging.2

    Only a few days after Petrie arrived, and maybe a little prematurely, his assistant James Quibell, wrote to James Henry Breasted, the Professor of Egyptology and Oriental History and founder of the Oriental Institute at the University of Chicago, giving him a status update on how the excavations were going.     We hope for papyri, we hope for cuneiform tablets; as yet of course it is early days and we have [found] nothing important.3

    What Petrie discovered is that in building his own mortuary temple, Merneptah raided Amenhotep III‚Äôs mortuary temple for parts.

    Because of this looting, Petrie was not a Merneptah fan, and he didn‚Äôt pull any punches when it came to describing his work. Referring to Merneptah Petrie wrote,

    But, it wasn‚Äôt all bad. At least not for Petrie.

    When you enter Merneptah‚Äôs Mortuary Temple, you‚Äôre standing in what‚Äôs called the First Court. When Petrie was excavating it he found at the back left corner of the court (that‚Äôs the south-west corner) a large, black, granite stele lying flat on the dirt floor.

    On the upward facing side they found an inscription written by Amenhotep III describing his various building projects including his mortuary temple, the Luxor temple, and the third Pylon at Karnak.9

    Clearly, the stele was one of the many items Merneptah had looted for his mortuary temple.

    ‚Äú‚Ä¶[the inscription] can only be seen a few inches from one‚Äôs nose as one lies under the stone‚Ä¶‚Äù10

    Beyond Apologetics

    Language of Annihilation

    It‚Äôs a bit like the Tel Dan Stele‚Äôs mention of the ‚ÄúHouse of David‚Äù - the apologetics stuff is interesting enough, but it‚Äôs when you look at the rest of the sentence that the famous phrase is in that questions start to appear. So let‚Äôs get into it.

    Merneptah

    As we‚Äôve seen, the Merneptah Stele‚Äôs mention of Israel is the phrase,

    ‚ÄúIsrael is wasted, its seed is not‚Äù17

    Now, there is plenty of discussion about what Merneptah meant by ‚Äúits seed is not‚Äù,18 but we‚Äôre not going to get into that in any detail other than to say that what happened to the ‚Äúseed‚Äù either means that ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù was completely wiped out down to the last man, woman and child, or, that their crops have been completely destroyed such that the few Israelites who survived the battle with Merneptah would die from starvation.     ‚Ä¶Merneptah claims to have utterly annihilated these people.19

    Clearly, annihilation isn‚Äôt all it‚Äôs cracked up to be. It seems being exterminated, wiped off the face of the earth, made non-existent, etc etc, was practically a guarantee that you were going to make a comeback, and probably in style.

    SCRIPTURE IS NO DIFFERENT IN THE WAY IT TALKS ABOUT ANNIHILATION.
