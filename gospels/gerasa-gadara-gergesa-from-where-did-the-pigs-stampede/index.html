    Gerasa, Gadara, Gergesa ‚Äì from where did the pigs stampede?

    12 minute read

    A friend recently asked about the geographical problem of Mark 5 so I thought I‚Äôd write up some thoughts on the topic.

    The narrative in question is the healing of Legion. Jesus is in Capernaum, he gets on a boat, sails across the Sea of Galilee, ends up in a storm, and calms the water. When they arrive on the shore he gets out and is met by two demoniacs/a guy called Legion. After Jesus heals him/them, the demons are sent into a herd of pigs. These demon-possessed pigs then run headlong into the lake and, presumably, drown. It‚Äôs fair to say this is one of the weirder gospel narratives.

    The healing of Legion is narrated in all three synoptic gospels; they all begin by describing where the event took place:

    Matthew 8:28 ‚ÄúWhen he came to the other side, to the country of the Gadarenes, two demoniacs coming out of the tombs met him.‚Äù1

    Mark 5:1 ‚ÄúThey came to the other side of the sea, to the country of the Gerasenes.‚Äù

    Luke 8:26 ‚ÄúThen they arrived at the country of the Gerasenes, which is opposite Galilee.‚Äù

    OK. First up: they don‚Äôt agree. Mark and Luke locate the event in ‚Äúthe country of the Gerasenes‚Äù; Matthew in ‚Äúthe country of the Gadarenes‚Äù ‚Äì that‚Äôs the geographical problem we‚Äôre going to look at in this post.

    These differences caused copyists of the gospels to perform no end of monkey-business with the sentence ‚Äì there are 3 variants for each of the synoptic gospels.2 We‚Äôre looking at a genuine problem that was recognised very early.

    If we accept Marcan Priority (which we do around here) then we‚Äôve got Mark writing ‚Äúcountry of the Gerasenes‚Äù, Luke following him faithfully, but Matthew deliberately changing ‚ÄúGerasenes‚Äù to ‚ÄúGadarenes‚Äù in his version of events. Why would Matthew do that?

    Gerasa, one of the cities of Decapolis and the capital of the country of the Gerasenes, lay around 37 miles/60 kilometres away from the Sea of Galilee in what is today the city of Jerash in Jordan.3 The country of the Gerasenes did not reach the Sea of Galilee (an important point that we‚Äôll get to in a bit).

    The narratives of Mark and Luke tell us that the heard of pigs was located ‚Äúon the hillside‚Äù and that ‚Äúthe herd‚Ä¶ rushed down the steep bank into the sea‚Äù (Mk 5:13 ‚Äì Luke‚Äôs record is pretty much identical). If you know the geography of the Sea of Galilee at all, it‚Äôs almost entirely surrounded by hills, some of them pretty steep. Interpreting the reference to ‚Äúthe hillside‚Äù as anywhere but the steep banks that encircle the lake is geographically problematic: the record describes a pretty short run for the pigs ‚Äì their stampede was quickly over.

    This is a difficulty for Mark‚Äôs account ‚Äì the pigs would have had to trot many miles from the country of the Gerasenes before they even reached the hillside that overlooks the lake, never mind actually get to the lake itself. And that doesn‚Äôt fit the description we‚Äôre given of the stampede at all.

    Matthew, using Mark as a source for his own gospel, appears to have been aware of this geographic difficulty. To deal with it he changed ‚Äúcountry of the Gerasenes‚Äù to ‚Äúcountry of the Gadarenes‚Äù:

    Matthew 8:28 ‚ÄúWhen he came to the other side, to the country of the Gadarenes, two demoniacs coming out of the tombs met him.‚Äù

    Why would he do that? Why swap out one territory for another?

    The city of Decapolis whose territory stood between the country of the Gerasenes and the Sea of Galilee was Gadara, modern Um-Qais in Jordan. Gadara was the capital of Matthew‚Äôs ‚Äúcountry of the Gadarenes‚Äù. Josephus explains that the country of the Gadarenes reached the shore of the lake:

    ‚Äúthe villages of the Gadarenes‚Ä¶ happened to lie on the frontier between Tiberias and the territory of the Scythopolitans.‚Äù4

    So at least that part of the story adds up ‚Äì the pigs could have run into the lake from Gadara‚Äôs territory. However, check out this map:

    Gadara itself or anywhere relatively close to it is still quite a distance ‚Äì a number of miles/kilometres. The pigs would still have had to have been long distance runners ‚Äì not the picture painted for us in Mark or Luke where the herd is on the hillside close to the water.

    It‚Äôs interesting then that Matthew locates the pigs not ‚Äúon the hillside‚Äù from where they could have ‚Äúrushed down the steep bank into the sea‚Äù. Here‚Äôs where Matthew locates the pigs:

    Mt 8:30 Now a large herd of swine was feeding at some distance from them.

    Let‚Äôs be clear: I know zero greek. Like, not even the alphabet. So, I‚Äôm kinda dependent on lexicons to tell me what‚Äôs what. The ‚Äúat some distance‚Äù is a bit fluffy in the english ‚Äì it could mean anywhere from ‚Äúnot far‚Äù to ‚Äúmiles away‚Äù. So, all a layman like me who wants to avoid ‚Äúexegeting the english‚Äù can do is trust good scholarship:

    • BDAG: ‚Äúbe far away from someone or something‚Äù5
    • Louw & Nida: ‚Äúa position at a relatively great distance from another position‚Äî‚Äòfar, at a distance, some distance away, far away.‚Äô‚Äù6

    It‚Äôs comforting to know that Luz translates it ‚ÄúBut far off from them a large herd of pigs was feeding.‚Äù7 On the other hand, Newman and Stine make the astute observation that ‚ÄúIt seems that ‚Äòat some distance‚Äô does not indicate a great distance, since the pigs can be seen.‚Äù8

    All I‚Äôm able to say with any confidence is that Matthew felt compelled to change the location of the pigs. He could have followed Mark, but he chose to do something else.9 It seems reasonable to this layman to suggest that maybe Matthew relocated the herd because he knew enough of the geography to say that if this event took place in the country of the Gadarenes the herd couldn‚Äôt be both on a hillside and close to the shore ‚Äì Gadara was around 5 or 6 miles away from the shore, and the hills that lead from Gadara to the lake are very close to Gadara and a number of miles from the lake.

    So, Matthew is aware that on the face of it Mark‚Äôs account doesn‚Äôt work. He therefore took it upon himself to change the geographical details to make them more realistic. To get any further with working out where the event took place (if it took place at all) we can turn to church history.

    Eusebius of Caesarea (260‚Äì313 CE10) in his Onomasticon (basically a long list of place names with explanations of their location), not content with his available options of Gerasa and Gadara Eusebius decided to add a third candidate:

    Eusebius, Onomasticon, 74: Gergesa ‚Äì There the Lord healed the demoniacs. Now a village is pointed out beside Lake Tiberias, into which the swine rushed down headlong.11

    Gergasa, it has been suggested12, is a transliteration of Kursi, located half way up the eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee.

    Scholarship seems to be split on whether Gergasa/Kursi is a viable option or not:

    • ‚Äú‚Ä¶a site named Khersa (or Kursi) among the cliffs overlooking the lake‚Äîthe ‚Äútraditional‚Äù site, but with little to argue in its favor.13
    • ‚ÄúThe ‚ÄúGerasa‚Äù of Mark 5:1 was probably Kursi/Gergesa‚Äù14

    But, what‚Äôs the historical value of Eusebius‚Äô claim? After all it‚Äôs not far off 300 years after the events.

    Well, it seems Eusebius didn‚Äôt arrive at his ‚ÄúGergesa‚Äù idea alone; it appears he may have just been following Origen‚Äôs (185-253 CE) commentary on John where we read:

    Origen, John. 6.211. ‚ÄúBut Gergesa, from which comes the name the Gergesenes, is an ancient city in the vicinity of the lake which is now called Tiberias. There is a cliff lying beside this lake from which they point out the swine were cast down by the demons.‚Äù15

    That‚Äôs a pretty early source, and he‚Äôs appealing to an earlier tradition (‚Äúfrom which they point out‚Ä¶‚Äù) but whether the entry really reflects ancient tradition is hard to know.

    One thing‚Äôs for sure: Gergasa, if it can be equated with Kursi, is ‚Äúthe most geographically plausible‚Äù16 reading. Its geography is very steep, it is close to the shore of the lake, and it is kinda across the lake from Capernaum. Geographically it‚Äôs certainly a better fit than Gerasa or Gadara.

    When it comes down to it we really have no idea where the event the narratives are based on took place. It‚Äôs just one of those things. We‚Äôll probably never find out what the real deal is, but, as usual with these things, that‚Äôs only a problem if we subscribe to the patently false doctrine of inerrancy.17

    So‚Ä¶how did this problem arise? Was Mark‚Äôs knowledge of the Decapolis shaky? Did he just take a stab at a location that seemed possible based on what he‚Äôd heard somewhere? That‚Äôs quite possible and I certainly don‚Äôt dismiss that as an explanation for the geographical problem that prompted this post. However, I‚Äôm not alone in thinking that this isn‚Äôt the best explanation.1819 What I find a more credible suggestion is that given by Marcus in his commentary on Mark:

    Although ‚ÄúGerasa‚Äù is difficult geographically, it is appropriate symbolically, since the Hebrew root gr≈° means ‚Äúto banish‚Äù and is a common term for exorcism.20

    If Marcus is right then here‚Äôs an oversimplified explanation of what may have happened:

    Mark, wanting to make a theological point, locates the event in a place whose name is associated with casting out demons ‚Äì the language, as Marcus points out, does kinda support this.21 This strengthens the exorcism theme of the pericope‚Äì seems legit. A few years later, Matthew, using Mark as a source for his own gospel, either misses Mark‚Äôs theological point or wants to achieve something else with his text and attempts to ‚Äúcorrect‚Äù the event‚Äôs location. He deals with a remaining issue by locating the herd ‚Äúsome distance away‚Äù rather than on the hillside next to the lake. Around 150 years later Origen comes along, and, knowing that Matthew‚Äôs attempted fix isn‚Äôt watertight, relocates the event to Gergasa based on what is probably an ancient tradition.

    That‚Äôs just this layman‚Äôs 2 cents.

    Conclusion: we just don‚Äôt know where this event is supposed to have taken place.

    But one thing‚Äôs for sure: wherever the event happened it was a waste of good bacon.
