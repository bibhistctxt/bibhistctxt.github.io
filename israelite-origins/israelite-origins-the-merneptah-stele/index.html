    News of the stele caused a sensation. Only two days after his lecture, readers of the Yorkshire Post3 were given the highlights by the journalist who‚Äôd attended Flinders Petrie‚Äôs lecture:

    The first mention of the ‚ÄúChildren of Israel‚Äù discovered upon any monument of ancient Egypt has been brought to light as one of the results of the recent labours of Professor Flinders Petrie‚Ä¶ this mention is quite brief, but perfectly clear.4

    Discovery

    A few weeks earlier Flinders Petrie had been in Egypt, excavating temples in Thebes on Luxor‚Äôs west bank over the winter of 1895-1896.     The site of Merenptah‚Äôs temple was disastrously dull; there were worn bits of soft sandstone, scraps looted from the temple of Amanhetep III, crumbling sandstone sphinxes, laid in pairs in holes to support columns.5

    Though the end of his reign was peaceful enough Merneptah ruled during the beginning of the Late Bronze Age collapse and he suffered the first waves of the invasions of the Sea People.     [Merneptah] must have realized that he did not have many years left, however, for his mortuary temple on the Theban West Bank is constructed almost exclusively from blocks removed from earlier structures, particularly the nearby temples of Amenhotep III.6

    Elsewhere Flinders Petrie wrote that the destruction and looting Menreptah had inflicted on his predecessor Amenhotep III‚Äôs mortuary temple was,

    ‚Ä¶as bad as anything ever done by Turk or Pope‚Ä¶7

    Quite the statement. As disappointed with Merneptah‚Äôs temple as Flinders Petrie was, the discovery of the Stele was the highlight of the expedition. It was found face down,

    ‚Ä¶in the S.W. corner of the first court. It had evidently stood against the south wall in the corner, and been overthrown forwards. It rested on the base of the column at its east side.8

    The stele was originally Amenhotep III‚Äôs. On it he described his building projects that included his mortuary temple, the Luxor temple, and the third Pylon at Karnak.9 Many years later Merneptah took the Stele and had his own inscription written on the back of it.

    Flinders Petrie had the ground below the stele shovelled out creating a small space to crawl into. Some of Flinders Petrie‚Äôs notebooks are publicly available; here‚Äôs a transcript of what he wrote on the stele‚Äôs discovery where he mentions this small space:

    ‚Ä¶Then on the other side of this great stele Merenptah has cut a long inscription of his own. This I have not yet looked over as it can only be seen a few inches from one‚Äôs nose as one lies under the stone; but I must copy it soon.10

    ‚Ä¶lay there copying for an afternoon, and came out saying, ‚ÄúThere are names of various Syrian towns, and one which I do not know, Isirar.‚Äù ‚ÄúWhy, that is Israel,‚Äù said I. ‚ÄúSo it is, and won‚Äôt the reverends be pleased,‚Äù was his reply.11

    And so the significance of the stele was established; the earliest mention of ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù outside the bible.

    Spiegelberg wasted no time and published his transcription the same year.12 Here‚Äôs the pertinent bit, found on line 27 of the inscription:

    The mention of Israel comes almost at the end of the stele in a list of places and towns Merneptah had fought. Here‚Äôs Spiegelberg‚Äôs translation:

    The princes bend down, saying ‚ÄòHail!‚Äô Not one raises his head among the Nine Bows.     So, who or where is ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù?

    Merneptah‚Äôs Israel ‚Äì a people or a place?

    Since the discovery of the Merneptah Stele, a considerable amount of noise has been made about the fact that its mention of ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù, in contrast to Canaan, Ashkelon, Gezer, Yenoam, and Hurru, is given the people determinative instead of the place determinative. In other words, whoever wrote the inscription thought of ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù as a people rather than a place. Petrie explained this in an 1896 article as follows:

    That the name here is that of the people Israel, and not of the city Jezreel, is shown by the writing of it with s and not z, and by its being expressly a ‚Äúpeople‚Äù unlike the other names here, which are those of ‚Äúplaces.‚Äù16

    This reasoning, along with its implications for what it means for understanding the relevant biblical narratives, remains the same today. For example, here‚Äôs an excerpt from Hoffmeier‚Äôs entry for the Merneptah Stele in Context of Scripture:

    It has long been noted that the writing of Israel uses the determinative (semantic indicator) for an ethnic group, and not for a geographic region or city. This scenario is in complete agreement with the picture portrayed in the books of Joshua and Judges, viz. the Israelites had no clearly defined political capital city, but were distributed over a region.17

    The same reasoning about the determinative can be found across the spectrum18, from very conservative19, conservative20, middle-of-the-road, 212223and even minimalist works24.

    The thing is, it‚Äôs probably not that clear cut. As Miller points out, the people determinative is used on this same stele for places, specifically,

    A thorough examination of Egyptian scribal practice, however, shows the use of the determinative to be almost completely arbitrary‚Ä¶ Within the Merneptah Stele itself, in lines 4‚Äì5 the Meshwesh, who are definitely a people, have the city-state determinative; in line 5 and line 10 the Libyan people (rbw) have the city-state determinative; and in lines 11 and 21 Libya (Tjehenu [thnw]) has both the people determinative and the city-state determinative. The determinatives should not be overread for ‚Äúwhat they say about Israel.‚Äù25

    If that is the case, and plenty seem to agree26, then insisting that the Israel of the Merneptah Stele refers to a people group rather than a city/location is probably going too far.

    Locating Merneptah‚Äôs Israel

    What‚Äôs more insightful is William Dever‚Äôs argumentation working out where Israel, in the mind of the scribe, was located.27 Let‚Äôs go through that now.

    As we‚Äôve already mentioned, ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù appears in the final section of the Stele, a section which records Merneptah‚Äôs campaign in Canaan ‚Äì fictional or otherwise.28 That helps pin down the area in which Israel, people or place, must have been located ‚Äì somewhere in the southern Levant. So, where was it?

    Well, according to Dever here‚Äôs where Israel couldn‚Äôt have been:

    1. The coast and lowlands were run by Egyptian vassal city states, like Ashkelon and Gezer.
    2. Galilee, being the northern part of Canaan, was under Egyptian control
    3. The southern Negev, according to other New Kingdom Egyptian texts was never conquered, nor did it need to be.

    That leaves the central hill country. And Dever‚Äôs not alone in this. Miller makes the same point:

    The Merneptah Stele is direct positive evidence that the term ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù was used for some entity in the highlands of Palestine in the parlance of Late Bronze IIb sources.29

    However, Noll makes the case that Israel were/was not in the central hill country:

    Israel‚Ä¶ apparently, lived in the vicinity of Ashkelon, Gezer and Yanoam, thus, perhaps the Cisjordan Highlands or the Jezreel Valley. The latter is more likely since, from archaeological surveys, it would seem that very few people were living in the hills of Canaan. It is not impossible that Merneptah did battle with the few people who were living in the hills at that time. It would have been a short and one-sided battle! I suspect, however, that Israel was part of the lowland population.30

    However, Dever‚Äôs response to this sort of argument is pretty strong:

    Is it merely a coincidence that most of the 13th‚Äì12th century B.C. villages recently brought to light by archaeology are located precisely [in the central hill country]?31

    If the people living in the villages that make up the Israelite Settlement pattern we looked at in the previous post were the ancestors of people who lived 200 years later that we‚Äôre all happy to call ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù, it kinda makes sense to associate Merneptah‚Äôs ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù with either the central hill country (or a part of it), or with a group of people living in it. Miller explains this better than I can:

    ‚Ä¶it makes no difference what the Iron I highlanders called themselves: they were the direct antecedents of Iron II Israel and, thus, ‚ÄúProto-Israel.‚Äù There is direct continuity from the Iron I highlands to Iron II Israel and Judah in pottery, settlements, architecture, burial customs, and metals‚Ä¶ So whatever the Iron I highlanders called themselves, by their continuity with Iron II they were nevertheless ‚Äúthose elements that were not yet Israel, but which went into or led up to the creation of Israel‚Äù (Thompson 1987:33). Yet since the Merneptah Stele records that the name of this community, or at least part of it, was Israel, once archaeology has established the continuity to Iron II, there is no reason to retain the prefix ‚ÄúProto-.‚Äù32

    Anyway, regardless of whether Merneptah‚Äôs ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù is a place or a people, and regardless of whether it was/they were located in the central hill country or the Jezreel Valley, what actually matters is this:

    According to Merneptah, defeating Israel was something worth writing home about.

    Pretty much any scholar writing about the stele makes this point. Here are a few examples:

    Israel was no less significant than Ashkelon and Gezer, two of the more important city-states in Palestine at the time.33

    This Israel was well enough established by that time among the other peoples of Canaan to have been perceived by Egyptian intelligence as a possible challenge to Egyptian hegemony.34

    Israel functioned as an agriculturally-based/sedentary socioethnic entity in the late 13th century B.C., one that is significant enough to be included in the military campaign against political powers in Canaan.35

    I could go on, but I won‚Äôt. You‚Äôre not paying me for this.

    The point is clear: if Merneptah felt that defeating Israel was as big a deal as defeating the mighty cities of Ashkelon and Gezer, then ‚ÄúIsrael‚Äù wasn‚Äôt an insignificant entity. It must have been a relatively big deal, at least in the context of Late Bronze Age Canaan.

    A fly in the ointment

    There‚Äôs one problem with all this: the Israelite Settlement Pattern we went through in the previous post didn‚Äôt begin until maybe 50-70 years after Merneptah‚Äôs mention of Israel. That makes it hard to demonstrate a one-to-one correspondence between Merneptah‚Äôs Israel and those who settled the Canaanite highlands.
